Exotic dance research page

Exoticism is better approached through language facts than hard facts. It is not unique to specific places, peoples, or objects but instead refers to a way of looking at and discoursing about all three. It is a discursive formation whereby the West constructs a geographic Other and thus its identity. In all constructs of Otherness, the Subject (or the Self) stigmatizes a difference (geographic, in this case), essentializes it, and on the basis of that essentialization, negates the identity of the Other. This process often legitimizes discrimination. Exoticism is by nature asymmetrical: the Self (in here) has the power to name and place the Other (out there) but not the other way around. Exoticism is also reductive and deterministic as it essentializes difference and relegates Otherness to a space from which the Other derives its essential traits. The exotic, therefore, is the faraway, or rather, that which is far away from us, typically warm, tropical countries. Europe, for instance, would not be exotic to Peruvians or Senegalese. That explains why in the past, “exotic products” stores sold goods from colonies and why today, expressions such as “exotic fruit” or “exotic wood” refer exclusively to tropical plants.
